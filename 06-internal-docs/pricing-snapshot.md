## PART 2 — THE LUMINOUS STEWARD PRICING FRAMEWORK

### The Only Frame That Matters

Anissa does not sell time. She sells the conditions for something that cannot be scheduled or guaranteed — but that she has spent eight years learning how to hold.

That has a price that is distinct from the price of a massage.

The mistake most practitioners make is pricing the technique. Sixty minutes, seventy-five dollars. The technique is priced correctly. The presence, the reading, the eight years of listening — those are not in the calculation at all.

When Anissa prices only the technique, she is giving the presence away.

### What "The Sanctuary" Actually Costs to Build

Eight years at the rate of approximately 20 sessions per week, 45 weeks per year:

8 × 20 × 45 = 7,200 sessions before she became who she is now.

7,200 sessions of holding, reading, responding, recalibrating. That is not counted in the hourly rate. It should be.

## PART 4 — THE GAP MATH

### What This Practice Is Worth at Correct Rates

Current estimate (48 clients, estimated 2 sessions/month average, $90/session):

48 × 2 × $90 = **$8,640/month gross**

At recommended rates (same client load, $125 average blended):

48 × 2 × $125 = **$12,000/month gross**

The difference is $3,360 per month — $40,320 per year — for the same 48 clients, the same 96 sessions per month, the same room, the same hands.

That gap does not require new clients. It does not require marketing. It requires a number that reflects the work.

### The Real Number

If Anissa moves to recommended rates and adds 12 new clients over the next 12 months (1 per month — reasonable given the referral quality of her existing base):

60 × 2 × $125 = **$15,000/month gross**

This is not a projection. It is arithmetic.

## PART 5 — HOW TO RAISE RATES (WITHOUT A SCRIPT)

### The Core Principle

Anissa does not owe anyone an explanation for what her work costs. She owes them honesty — and this is the honest number.

### Existing Clients — The Transition

For people who have been coming at current rates:

1. Give 30 days' notice of the rate change
2. Offer one final session at the current rate if the relationship warrants it
3. Do not apologize in the language of the message
4. Send this, in her own voice:

---

*I want to let you know that my rates are changing [date] — 60-minute sessions will move to $110, and 90-minute sessions to $145.*

*Eight years of practice, and I have never raised my rates. It is time.*

*If you would like to book one more session at the current rate before [date], I am holding space for that. After that, the new rate applies.*

*I am grateful for the trust you bring into the room. That has not changed and will not.*

*Anissa*
*843-283-8228*

---

### New Clients

New clients book at the new rates. No explanation needed, no transition period. The rate is on the booking information. They agree to it by showing up.

### The Felt-Sense Check

When Anissa quotes her rate to someone new, she should feel the number land with the same steadiness she brings to the room. If she feels the urge to add "but I'm flexible" or "for a first session I can do" — that is the place to pause.

The rate is the rate because the work is the work.

## PART 7 — WHAT IS NOT BEING SAID HERE

This is not a document that tells Anissa to hustle, scale, or optimize. The practice does not need those things.

What it needs is a rate that is honest. A rate that does not make Anissa subsidize other people's access to work she has spent eight years building. A rate that lets her sustain the practice without burning out, without adding sessions she does not have space for, without quietly resenting the math.

The Luminous Steward holds the lamp. She does not burn herself to light the room.

**That is the only reason any of this matters.**
